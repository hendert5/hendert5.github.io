EagleCam: a 1.5U Low-Cost CubeSat Mission for a Novel Third-Person View of a Lunar Landing

The interest in returning to the Moon for research and exploration has increased as new tipping point technologies are providing the potential for safer access to the lunar surface. NASA's Artemis program, for example, plans to return humans to the lunar surface by 2025 to study water deposits on the lunar south pole. This program will also serve as a critical milestone for technology development and programmatic operations for manned Martian surface exploration. For humanity's safe return to the lunar surface, advances in technology and knowledge of the lunar surface environment are required.

Ahead of the United States' Artemis missions, Intuitive Machines (IM) received in 2019 a NASA Commercial Lunar Payload Services (CLPS) contract and will land the Nova-C lunar lander in early 2023. In his 2019 Alumni SpeakER Series talk entitled, "Returning to the Moon: A New Era of Lunar Exploration", Steve Altemus, President and Chief Executive Officer of IM, challenged the students of Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University (ERAU) to build a camera system to capture the first-ever third-person view of a lunar landing. Accepting the challenge, students within the university's Space Technologies Laboratory, located within the John Mica Engineering and Aerospace Innovation Complex, generated the idea for what would become EagleCam

While taking the third-person view remains the primary objective, there still exists a significant amount of scientific return that will aid in the understanding of the lunar surface environment directly surrounding the Nova-C landing location. For example, EagleCam will be the first device to test Wi-Fi in the lunar surface to transmit wirelessly all the data from the computer located inside the CubeSat to our secondary computer located in the deployer.

LLAMAS (Literally Looking At More Astronauts in Space)

As the number of launches to space increase, commercial space exploration is becoming more common as a pathway to do more research and enable access to space. Jared Issacman, ERAU alumni and astronaut started with the Inspiration 4 mission, which led to the Polaris Program. "The Polaris Program is a first-of-its-kind effort to rapidly advance human spaceflight capabilities, while continuing to raise funds and awareness for important causes here on Earth‚Äù. With this mindset, Issacman ask the team at the Space Technologies Laboratory to built a device for his first mission Polaris Dawn that would test science in space. This first mission will fly in a SpaceX Dragon capsule and will perform the first commercial Extra Vehicular Activity (EVA). It will have many health experiments and will test and validate SpaceX first suits made for outer space.

Based on the knowledge and expertise from crafting EagleCam, the team at STL took on the challenge of creating a device that will provide an experience unique of its kind by creating a VR experience using a stereoscopic camera system to record the EVA and to provide feedback to SpaceX of their suit by using a thermal camera. The LLAMAS mission includes additionally a radiation dosimeter and will test new hardware for one of their partners in space by evaluating the performance of their commercial-of-the-shelf components.
